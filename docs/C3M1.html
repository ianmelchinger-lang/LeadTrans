Module C3 · M1 — Choose your AI operating model

Operating Model Drift

The situation. AI is everywhere in the enterprise: pilots in several units, licenses in more, a tool that shines in one team and breaks in another. Early gains came fast, then flattened. The board has started asking what all of it is returning — and there is no clean answer.

The reflex is to buy another platform. But the real cause is structural: no one chose how AI is owned. Fixing it starts with drawing a boundary. The module puts it bluntly — the failure modes live at this line.

Part 1 · Practice

Sort each capability to the side that should own it

Hub-and-spoke is the most-used way to organize AI: a central team (the hub) owns the shared foundations, while each business unit (a spoke) runs the AI work close to its own customers. For each item below, choose whether the hub owns it or each unit owns it. The rule the module gives: foundations sit at the center, delivery sits at the edge. Balanced hub-and-spoke

Foundations at the center, delivery at the edge. The shared platform and governance hold, while units move fast on their own use cases. This is the separation of concerns the model is built for.

Every operating-model choice comes back to this line. A hub that claims too much forms queues and drives builders underground. Units that claim too much let the shared view go dark. You move the line deliberately, with the organization's maturity — not by default.
