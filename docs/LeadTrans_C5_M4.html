Build the honest adoption rate

A license report can show almost everyone activated and still tell you nothing about whether the work changed. This exercise practices the fix: computing an adoption rate against the right people and the right definition of use, instead of the easy numbers everyone already has on hand.

The snapshot

Initiative: an AI drafting assistant rolled out to a claims-processing team. Reporting period: one quarter. These are the facts on hand.

Licensed users — issued access to the tool 200
Recorded events — people who logged at least one use during the quarter 130
Of the 200 licensed users, those with no genuine claims task for the tool this quarter — on leave, moved teams, or handling case types the tool doesn't cover 40
Of the 130 with a recorded event, those who also completed the required compliance review on their AI-assisted draft 110

What's already circulating on the steering committee's slide:

"130 of 200 licensed users generated a recorded event — 65% adoption."

That figure divides recorded events by every licensed person. It shows how many people tried the tool at least once. It does not show whether the tool changed anyone's actual work — and it counts people who never had a real task for it against the same yardstick as people who used it properly.

Your turn: build the honest rate

The module calls this the qualified-use rate: qualified users, divided by the opportunity-aware population — the people who genuinely had a relevant task this quarter, not just anyone with a license. A qualified-use event is more than one logged interaction; here it means the draft was used and the required review was completed.

One rate is not the whole story. This tells you access turned into real, reviewed use this quarter. It doesn't yet tell you whether that use keeps happening next quarter — that's workflow integration — or whether it moved a business result — that's outcome. Each of those needs its own evidence before anyone calls this a win.
